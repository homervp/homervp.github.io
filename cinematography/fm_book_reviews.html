Film making book reviews

In recent times I have developed an exponential appreciation for the art and craft of storytelling, in particular via film. As there are numerous books on the subject, with a focus on various specializations, having very different approaches and tones, I decided to share my impressions of various books I ended up reading. Some books are packed full of information and stick to the technical aspect, others are more of a diary or collection of personal experiences, and will be less dense, but might still be very insightful. Others yet are very focused, but so formulaic that they only really teach you how to make the same film over and over...

Save the cat: The last book on screenwriting you will ever need

Formulaic to a fault, and overconfident at it.

I want to get something annoyingly obvious about the title. If you checked the book out on any website, you probably saw in the related recommendations books like "Save the cat writes horror", "Save the cat writes a novel", "Save the cat writes a young adult novel". This is clearly not the last book you will ever need, otherwise these books would not have been published after it. End of rant, but blatantly false advertising always makes my eye twitch.

With that out the way, on to the book. The book will guide you in writing a screenplay from start to finish, giving you a proper structure, tips on evaluating your ideas, what to do when you are stuck, and all that. If you follow this book, you will write a screenplay. Possibly an acceptable one (which is as much as you can really expect for your first screenplay).

I have two caveats. The first one is the absolutist way the rules are stated. I can forgive that, as students, especially those in self-study, tend to underestimate the importance of what they're told. I would recommend, every time the author states a rule, think of movies that broke it, and consider whether they needed that rule. The very good ones are not good for breaking the rule, they broke the rule and remained good because their writer really knew what they were doing, and could make a consious choice. In my opinion it is a good thing to break these rules, as long as you look back and evaluate whether the end result is good, or you need to re-write. That's how you get good, and that's how you appreciate them.

The second one is, this book is way too formulaic and one-dimensional! It will provide you with a template for your screenplay, that has very specific story beats, very specifically placed on a very specific page number. The book will very explicitly instruct you to not deviate by more than one page from that number. To me, this is unnecessary. It is certainly an interesting exercise, to try to fit a story into a box of a specific shape (we are doing that every time we write a screenplay), but there are great films at under 70 minutes, and there are great films at over 2 hour (though arguably an inexperienced writer should err on the side of caution and not bet the whole endeavour on keeping the audience's attention for 3 hours.

A final note. During the book, the author is very clear on his goals. He wants to sell speculative scripts. Speculative scripts, or specs, are scripts that studios buy and keep on the shelf to maybe produce at a later time. This is fine, not all scripts will find the producer who will click and want to make it right away. But making this your goal is an unhealthy mentality to me. I believe every writer should hope to see their story put to the big screen, for what is the point of a story, if not to be told and shared with the world? I'm not an idealist, I will gladly sell the script as a spec rather than not sell it at all, but the mentality of the book is to consistently write a consistent average and predictable family comedy (I mean it, that's the author's specialization and it is explicitly stated), because it sells as a spec, rather than help you write something new and original

Verdict

Save the Cat offers a very comprehensive coverage of screenwriting for an absolute beginner. However people who know a bit more than what a 3-act structure is, might not have that much to learn, and might find themselves suffocating under the formulaic and narrow-sighted directionality of the book. This is definitely not the last book you will ever need, but it could well be the first.

Screenplay: The foundations of Screenwriting (Syd Field)

A solid foundation

Screenplay offers a very solid and concrete foundation for writing a 3-act structure from the man who identified the 3-act structure as a significant format of storytelling. Syd Field has taught screenplay for over two decades, and sorted through submitted screenplays for Cinemobile. The book is packed with expereinces from these times.

Though I cannot say the book is extremely dense in individual lessons, each point is very well and thoroughly analyzed, leaving it very clear in the reader's mind. This includes the 3-act structure with some special attention to the two turning points That separate the acts, but also tying the beginning and the end, writing solid characters, writing good scenes, and so on.

I frankly don't have a lot to say about the book. I will say that if you write down notes while reading, you might find that they are not quite as many as you expected, but that is more so because they are very well explained than a lack of content. The book focuses on quality rather than quantity and I recommend it.

How to shoot a feature film for under $10k (and not go to jail)

This is one of the most thorough all-rounders in the market. Reading this book you will learn about everything from writing and casting, to handling lighting and sound, efficient equipment rental, shoot scheduling, managing a DIY production and managing morale on the set, and some post-processing tips.

If you have no idea about the technical aspects of film-making, this is an amazing point to start. There are even breakdowns and checklists of things to keep in mind. I have brought this book to short film sets as something to check back to if something unexpected comes - I never actually had to pull it out, but it was comforting to know it's in by backpack.

Highly recommended

Making Films (Sydney Lumet)

Sydney Lumet was a name I hadn't noticed until I checked out the book, but he has quite a few exceptionally rated timeless classics under his name, my favorite being "12 angry men" (rated at a pretty insane 9.0 on IMDB).

This is again a not-super-dense book, as Sydney ties each lesson to a story from his productions, but it imparts some very strong lessons. It remains the most memorable explanation of how impactful the deliberate choice of lens is, as well as the height of the camera's position relative to the subject is. He covers points such as reading the script, working with the writer, dealing with actors (many interesting stories there), managing the shooting schedule and the shoot itself and working with the editor.

This is a solid choice for the director who is conscious not only of the cinematographic aspect of directing, but also aspires to have a complete understanding of the well-oiled production process and how other people come into play in the final result.

Rebel without a crew (Robert Rodriguez)

This book is not so much instructional, as it is about learning from Robert's experience filming El Mariachi. The first half of the book is a curated version of his diary, where you see where he comes from, how he set his goal and deadlines, participating in a month-long pharmaceutical test to finance his film, gathering actors from acquaintances, planning and filming and dealing with issues during the shoot. While there are definitely things to learn, you will mostly come along the ride for the perspective and experience, and will benefit from the realization that you should "just do it" rather than wait until you are ready after film school or self-study or anything else.

The second half of the book is again a curated diary of his experiences getting picked up by Hollywood and dealing with the studios. This is an often hilarious, absurd and surreal experience, which I found interesting and entertaining.

Verdict

Although there is not quite as much technical knowledge, this book does teach more about the mentality that is needed to go and make something, and demonstrates how you can make something of magnitude even as an amateur. This, to me, is invaluable, however I cannot say I recommend it if you really have to pick just one or two books.
